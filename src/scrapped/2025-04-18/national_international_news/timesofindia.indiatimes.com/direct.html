Compassion in combat
1945
US National Archive
(Photographer)
TOI take:
Published on: Apr 12, 2025
On April 11, 1945, during the Battle of Okinawa, a Japanese kamikaze pilot crashed his Zero fighter into the USS Missouri, creating a fireball but causing no serious injuries among the crew. In an extraordinary gesture, Captain William Callaghan ordered a burial at sea with full military honours for the fallen pilot, treating him with the same respect as an American sailor. Eighty years later, the Missouri, now a museum in Pearl Harbor, commemorated this act of humanity with a ceremony honouring peace and reconciliation.
Remembering Rwanda: 100 days of horror, 30 years on
1994
Jean-Marc Bouju
(Photographer)
TOI take:
Published on: Apr 07, 2025
On April 7, 1994, following the assassination of Rwanda’s president, Hutu extremists began a 100-day massacre, killing hundreds of thousands of Tutsis and moderate Hutus. Gangs with machetes roamed the country, slaughtering people in homes, farms, churches, and schools. Survivors like Nyabimana bore deep wounds—physical and unimaginable—from the horrors.
Jallianwala Bagh massacre: The horror of survivors
1919
Narayan Vinayak Virkar
(Photographer)
TOI take:
Published on: Mar 12, 2025
This 1919 photograph captures witnesses of the Jallianwala Bagh massacre, with bullet holes circled in white as survivors point to them. British troops, under General Dyer, killed hundreds of peaceful protesters. While Dyer tried to silence dissent, this image became a powerful voice against British colonial atrocities.
